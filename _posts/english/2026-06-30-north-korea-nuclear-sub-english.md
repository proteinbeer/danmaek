North Korea has publicly revealed its construction of an 8,700-ton nuclear-powered strategic guided missile submarine, marking a significant escalation in the Korean peninsula's military competition. Leader Kim Jong Un personally inspected the construction site.

## North Korea's Submarine

The submarine, first announced at the 2021 Eighth Party Congress, is estimated to carry 10 submarine-launched ballistic missiles (SLBMs) in a sail-mounted configuration. Analysis suggests the vessel uses highly enriched uranium (above 90%), which would eliminate the need for refueling during its operational lifetime.

Professor Lim Eul-chul of Kyungnam University warned that "North Korea's submarines are no longer a hypothetical threat but an emerging reality that could threaten not only South Korea but also the US mainland."

## South Korea's Response

South Korea announced in May 2026 its own nuclear submarine development program, named the "Jang Bogo-N Project." President Lee Jae-myung secured explicit US approval from President Trump during their October 2025 summit, marking a major shift in US non-proliferation policy toward Seoul.

South Korea's submarines will use low-enriched uranium (approximately 20%), requiring refueling every 10 years. Defense officials emphasize this underscores Seoul's commitment to non-proliferation principles. Construction is expected to take up to 10 years, with vessels operating for over 30 years.

## US Diplomacy

US Under Secretary of State for Political Affairs Allison Hooker visited Seoul in early June for consultations on North Korea policy and South Korea's nuclear submarine plan. The visits highlight growing US-ROK coordination on maritime security.

## Strategic Implications

The parallel submarine programs signal a shift from land-based to underwater military competition on the Korean peninsula. North Korea's pursuit of sea-based nuclear deterrence and South Korea's quest for counter-capabilities are reshaping the regional security landscape.

South Korea, currently reliant on diesel-electric submarines, faces a critical capability gap as the North advances its undersea strike capabilities. The Korea Herald analysis suggests that "military competition between the two Koreas is set to intensify, while prospects for inter-Korean dialogue will further diminish."
