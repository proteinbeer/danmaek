South Korea's stock market experienced historic turbulence in late June 2026. The KOSPI index broke through 9,000 for the first time on June 22, driven by the AI semiconductor super-cycle, only to crash 9.99% the next day in the worst single-day drop on record.

## The 9,000 Rally

The rally was overwhelmingly driven by Samsung Electronics and SK Hynix, whose combined market capitalization now accounts for over 55% of the entire KOSPI — up from 35% at the start of the year. Both companies have been beneficiaries of the global AI investment boom, particularly in high-bandwidth memory (HBM) chips used in Nvidia's AI accelerators.

## 'Black Tuesday' and Aftermath

The June 23 crash — dubbed "Black Tuesday" — was triggered by profit-taking after the rapid rally, compounded by renewed US-Iran tensions. The KOSPI 200 Volatility Index (VKOSPI) surged to 97.78, the highest since official tracking began in 2009 and comparable to 2008 financial crisis levels.

The market has since seesawed wildly, with a 5.42% surge on June 25 followed by a 5.81% drop on June 26.

## Won-Dollar Exchange Rate

The Korean won has been trading in the 1,500-1,550 range against the US dollar, significantly weaker than the 1,100 won level seen a decade ago. The weak won benefits export-oriented companies like Samsung and Hyundai but raises import costs for energy and food, fueling domestic inflation.

## Structural Concerns

Market analysts point to two structural issues. First, the extreme concentration in two semiconductor stocks makes the entire market vulnerable to sector-specific shocks. Second, the weak won reflects broader competitiveness concerns — while Taiwan's dollar has remained stable against the USD over the past decade, the won has depreciated by over 30%.

## Outlook

July will be a critical month, with Samsung Electronics' preliminary Q2 earnings announcement expected on July 7. The US jobs report and CPI data will also influence market direction. Analysts advise focusing on KOSPI large-caps with visible earnings momentum while remaining cautious about the overheated semiconductor sector.
