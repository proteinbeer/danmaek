South Korea on June 29 laid out a sweeping industrial strategy centered on semiconductors and artificial intelligence, as President Lee Jae-myung unveiled over $576 billion (800 trillion won) in investments — the largest such commitment in the country's history.

## Three Pillars

The "Three Mega-Projects" consist of:

**1. AI Semiconductor Cluster.** Samsung Electronics and SK Hynix will build massive semiconductor fabrication facilities in the Honam (Gwangju) region, with combined investment exceeding 200 trillion won over 10 years.

**2. AI Data Center Infrastructure.** SK Group will construct gigawatt-scale AI data centers across five locations nationwide, starting with Ulsan. The plan includes securing over 260,000 AI accelerators in partnership with Nvidia.

**3. Physical AI.** Hyundai Motor Group and Samsung will invest in robotics, smart factories, and autonomous driving technology. Hyundai, which owns Boston Dynamics, is pursuing next-generation humanoid robot development combining its robotics expertise with Nvidia's AI platform.

## Global AI Ambitions

President Lee stated the goal is to propel South Korea into the world's top three AI powers alongside the US and China. The Ministry of Science and ICT announced a vision of "one AI agent per citizen" and launched a "K-Palantir" strategy targeting five AI unicorns by 2030.

## International Reaction

CNN, Reuters, and CNBC all covered the announcement prominently. CNN reported that "South Korea is making a bold bet on AI chip dominance with this $576 billion investment plan."

## Market Response and Challenges

While the announcement was historic, Samsung Electronics and SK Hynix shares actually declined on the news, suggesting the market had already priced in expectations. Critics point to practical challenges: the semiconductor cluster in Honam requires substantial new power grid, water, and workforce infrastructure that could take years to develop.

## Strategic Context

The investment comes amid a global chip race, with the US (CHIPS Act), China, Japan, and Europe all pouring resources into semiconductor self-sufficiency. South Korea, home to the world's two largest memory chip makers, is positioning itself to remain competitive in an increasingly contested industry.

Industry experts note that while the government's commitment is strong, execution will be key. "The convergence of government will and corporate execution capability will determine whether Korea's AI industry can achieve a quantum leap," one analyst commented.
