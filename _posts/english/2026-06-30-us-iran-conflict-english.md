The US and Iran have resumed hostilities in the Strait of Hormuz, throwing the May 17 ceasefire memorandum of understanding (MOU) into crisis. US Central Command struck 10 Iranian military targets for the second consecutive day, and President Trump warned of a "military finish."

## Incident Overview

CENTCOM said the strikes targeted Iranian reconnaissance infrastructure, communications systems, air defense bases, and drone storage facilities in direct response to attacks on commercial vessels. Trump posted on Truth Social that if the US can "no longer be reasonable, there may come a time when we need a military finish. In that case, Iran will no longer exist."

Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) retaliated by attacking eight US military facilities, including Camp Arifjan in Kuwait and the US Navy's Fifth Fleet base in Bahrain. The IRGC warned it would suspend all diplomatic procedures if US violations of the MOU continue.

## Three Korean Ships Still in the Strait

The clashes were triggered by Iran's drone attack on a Singapore-flagged container ship on June 25, followed by an attack on a Panama-flagged tanker on June 27. Three South Korean vessels remain trapped in the Strait of Hormuz, and the International Maritime Organization has suspended its evacuation plan.

## Doha Talks Agreed

Both sides have agreed to meet in Doha, Qatar on June 30 for negotiations focused on resolving the Strait of Hormuz dispute. Originally planned for Switzerland to discuss Iran's nuclear program, the venue was changed amid the escalating crisis.

A senior US official told Axios both sides agreed to cease all physical military operations.

## Impact on South Korea

The renewed conflict threatens South Korea's energy security and economic recovery. Oil prices, which had stabilized after the May MOU, are rising again. The three Korean ships in the Strait face an uncertain fate. Korea's export-driven economy is particularly vulnerable to disruptions in this critical waterway, through which about 20% of global oil passes.

The Wall Street Journal commented that "the fragile ceasefire agreement has been put to the test," noting that the core issue of Strait of Hormuz control was left unresolved in the original MOU.
