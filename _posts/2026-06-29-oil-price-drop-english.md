International oil prices have returned to pre-war levels following the US-Iran MOU on ceasefire, prompting the South Korean government to cut its gasoline ceiling price by 150 won per liter for the first time in 106 days.

## Oil Price Overview

Brent crude futures closed at $73.14 per barrel on June 25, recovering to pre-war levels. WTI fell to $69.92, and Dubai crude dropped to $67.29. At the peak of the conflict in March, Brent crude had surged to $110 per barrel.

The rapid stabilization followed the US-Iran memorandum of understanding that signaled an end to hostilities, which had disrupted shipping through the strategic Strait of Hormuz.

## Ceiling Price Adjustment

Starting June 27, South Korea lowered its petroleum ceiling prices by 150 won per liter across all fuel types: gasoline now capped at 1,784 won/L, diesel at 1,773 won/L, and kerosene at 1,380 won/L.

This marks the first reduction since the ceiling price system was introduced in March, when prices were actually raised by 210 won/L during the peak of the crisis.

## Why Gas Stations Haven't Lowered Prices Yet

Despite the drop in international crude, Korean gas stations are still charging around 2,000 won per liter. Three main factors explain the delay:

1. **Inventory lag**: Gas stations need 2-3 weeks to sell through high-cost inventory purchased before the price drop
2. **Post-settlement system**: Refiners set high baseline prices and later rebate stations, creating upward price stickiness
3. **Mine clearance**: The US Department of Defense estimates it will take 2-6 months to clear naval mines from the Strait of Hormuz

Experts expect pump prices to fall to the 1,800-won range by mid-July.

## Outlook

The Korea Energy Economics Institute projects that oil prices will begin declining in earnest from August. However, recovery of damaged production facilities in the Middle East could take over a year, while AI-driven energy demand continues to put upward pressure on prices.

"Full normalization is unlikely before the end of this year or early next year," an industry insider commented.
