Haitai Honey Butter Chip Review: Sweet, Salty, and Still Famous

Haitai Honey Butter Chip became one of Korea's most talked-about snacks after its 2014 launch. Early shortages and social-media attention turned the sweet-and-savory potato chip into a trend, but the product has remained on shelves long after the initial rush. Imported bags are usually priced above ordinary potato chips, so the flavor needs to be the main reason to choose it.

For context: this chip came out in August 2014, made by Haitai-Calbee (that's a joint venture between Korean Haitai and Japanese Calbee). It sold so fast stores had "sold out" signs up for months. At its peak, a single 60g bag was going for 50,000 won — like $50 USD — on Korean e-commerce sites. Fifty bucks for a bag of chips. Crazy, right?

First Impressions

The golden-yellow bag uses honey and butter imagery rather than an elaborate design. Once opened, the aroma leans toward warm butter and sweet baked goods more than plain salted chips. The exact ingredient list varies by market, so the package is the best source for butter, dairy, and allergen details.

The chips are thin, light golden, and dusted with a fine seasoning. Their crispness is closer to a delicate supermarket potato chip than a thick kettle-cooked style, with relatively little visible surface oil.

Taste and Texture

First bite: crisp. Clean snap, not greasy at all. The seasoning hits you right away — sweet honey up front, then this wave of buttery richness, and finally a subtle saltiness at the back that balances everything out.

The sweetness is noticeable but does not completely take over. Honey arrives first, followed by a warm, cookie-like butter note, while salt keeps the finish from becoming cloying. People who dislike sweet seasoning on savory snacks may still find it unusual.

The thin texture makes the chips easy to eat quickly. A 120g bag may contain roughly 690 calories depending on the market label, so checking the listed serving size is useful before treating the package as a single portion.

How It Compares

Compared with many honey-butter flavored chips, Haitai's version puts more emphasis on butter and less on sharp mustard or heavy sugar. Nongshim's honey-mustard products are tangier, while other export-market versions can taste sweeter. Availability varies considerably outside Korea.

At $4.99 it's definitely pricier than a standard bag of Lay's ($3-4). But hey, it's an import and you're paying for the real deal.

Nutrition

Per 60g serving (half the bag): 345 kcal, 22g fat (around 7g saturated), 34g carbohydrates, 4g sugar, and about 210mg of sodium. While the sodium is lower than your average savory potato chip, the fat content is relatively high due to the real butter blend, so it's a rich treat best enjoyed in moderation.

Verdict

Honey Butter Chip works because its three main notes — honey, butter, and salt — remain easy to distinguish. The thin chip keeps the seasoning from feeling too heavy, although the sweet profile will not suit everyone.

Best for: Snackers who enjoy sweet-and-salty combinations and want a lighter alternative to thick, heavily seasoned chips.

Where to buy: H Mart, Amazon, Korean grocery stores
