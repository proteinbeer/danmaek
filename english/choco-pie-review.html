Orion Choco Pie: The Snack That Defined Korean Childhoods

If there's one snack that every Korean person has grown up eating, it's Choco Pie. Since its launch in 1974 by Orion, Choco Pie has become more than just a snack — it's a nostalgic time capsule. School field trips, after-school snacks, lunchbox surprises — Choco Pie was always there.

Interestingly, Choco Pie also played a surprising role in geopolitics. During the 1990s, South Korea sent tons of Choco Pies to North Korea as humanitarian aid. They became so popular that North Korean soldiers reportedly traded them on the black market for up to $9.50 each. Some say Choco Pie access even influenced diplomatic negotiations at the Kaesong Industrial Complex. Who knew a snack could be that powerful?

The original Choco Pie was inspired by a Korean R&D team member who visited a hotel in Georgia, USA, in 1973. He was impressed by the chocolate-coated sweets at the hotel restaurant and brought the concept back to Korea. After months of experimentation, the Orion Choco Pie was born. It became so successful that the company eventually renamed itself from Tongyang Confectionery to Orion Confectionery.

What's Inside?

Two soft, spongy chocolate cake rounds with a fluffy marshmallow center, all enrobed in a thin layer of milk chocolate. The texture is the real star — the cake is moist and tender, almost like a brownie's softer cousin. The marshmallow gives a gentle chew without being sticky. And the chocolate shell adds just enough sweetness to tie everything together.

At about 130 calories per pie, it's not the lightest snack but it's also not the worst offender. The original version has been around for 50+ years with almost no recipe changes — if it ain't broke, don't fix it.

Let me break down the eating experience:

  • First bite: The chocolate shell cracks open, revealing the soft cake underneath — the contrast of textures is immediate and satisfying
  • The chew: The marshmallow center stretches slightly as you pull it apart, soft and pillowy without being overly sticky
  • The finish: The cake melts away, leaving a gentle chocolate aftertaste — clean, not cloying

Comparison: Orion vs Others

There are several Choco Pie variants out there. Lotte also makes one (Lotte Choco Pie), which is slightly sweeter and has a thinner cake layer. The Lotte version has a softer, almost gooey marshmallow center, while Orion's is firmer and more structured.

There's also a Japanese version called "Angel Pie" by Morinaga (available since 1961, even before the Korean version!), which has a different marshmallow-to-cake ratio. The Japanese version uses a thicker chocolate coating and a denser cake, making it feel more premium but less nostalgic.

In the US, you'll find Moon Pie (since 1917) and Wagon Wheels. Moon Pie uses graham cracker instead of chocolate cake and has a completely different texture — crunchier, less moist. Wagon Wheels are closer to Choco Pie but tend to be larger and sweeter.

But Orion's original remains the gold standard for Koreans — the cake is fluffier, the chocolate is richer, and the marshmallow has the perfect consistency. Here's how they compare:

Flavors Beyond the Original

Orion has released several limited edition Choco Pie flavors over the years:

  • Banana Choco Pie: A banana-flavored cream instead of the original marshmallow — surprisingly popular in China
  • Strawberry Choco Pie: Strawberry-flavored marshmallow with a pink coating — a Valentine's Day staple
  • Green Tea Choco Pie: Matcha-infused cake and white chocolate coating — for the trendy crowd
  • Choco Pie Ice Cream: A frozen version of the classic — exactly what it sounds like, and it's amazing

In Russia, Choco Pie has become so popular that Orion sells over 600 million packs annually there. Vietnam consumes another 600 million packs per year. Combined, that's over 1.2 billion Choco Pies sold in those two countries alone. The brand also controls a two-thirds share of the Chinese snack market.

Nutrition & Value

One Choco Pie (28g) has about 130 calories, 5g of fat (2.5g saturated), 19g of carbs (11g sugar), and 1g of protein. Not the healthiest snack by any stretch, but for an occasional treat, it's reasonable. A box of 12 costs about $6 at H Mart — that's 50 cents per pie.

Compared to other snack cakes like Twinkies (150 calories each) or Hostess CupCakes (180 calories each), Choco Pie is actually on the lighter side while being more satisfying thanks to the marshmallow center.

Verdict

Choco Pie is simple perfection. It's not trying to be gourmet or trendy. It's just a really well-made snack that has stood the test of time. Whether you're trying it for the first time or reliving childhood memories, Choco Pie delivers every single time.

What makes it special is the emotional connection. Every Korean person has a Choco Pie memory — sharing one with a friend during a school trip, getting one as a reward from a parent, or smuggling them across the DMZ (literally). It's not just a snack; it's a cultural artifact.

Would I buy again? I always have a box in my pantry. It's my go-to when I want something sweet but not too heavy, or when I'm feeling nostalgic.
