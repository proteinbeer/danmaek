Kit Kat Review: Japanese vs Australian Comparison

Kit Kat was born in Britain, but today it's sold in different flavors and forms around the world. Japan especially has turned it into a cultural phenomenon, releasing over 300 limited-edition flavors over the years. This time, I'm comparing the Australian original with the Japanese matcha Kit Kat.

The story goes back to 1935. A worker at Rowntree's factory in York, UK, suggested a "chocolate bar you could pack in a lunchbox," and that idea became Kit Kat. It launched as "Rowntree's Chocolate Crisp" and was renamed in 1937. Nestlé acquired Rowntree's in 1988, turning Kit Kat into the global brand it is today.

In South Korea, Lotte Confectionery produces Kit Kat under license, so you can find it easily at any convenience store. But Lotte's version tastes noticeably different from Nestlé-made Kit Kats found in other countries — the chocolate and wafer are quite distinct.

First Impression

The first thing you notice when you open the wrapper is the gentle cocoa aroma mixed with sweet milk chocolate. The Australian version comes in a red package, and the Japanese matcha one in green — sitting side by side, the color contrast is striking. The Australian Kit Kat is made by Nestlé, while the Japanese one was originally made by Fujiya (now also Nestlé).

Kit Kat's defining feature is its wafer structure. Most chocolate bars are just solid chocolate, but Kit Kat layers three wafers coated in chocolate, giving it that signature crisp, shattery crunch. That delicate sound and texture when you bite into it is seriously addictive.

Australian Original Kit Kat

The Australian Kit Kat is the Nestlé version. The biggest difference compared to the Lotte-made version sold in Korea is the chocolate intensity. The Australian version has a higher cocoa content in the milk chocolate, making it less sweet and slightly more bitter. The wafer is crisp, and the chocolate coating is thicker, so when you bite down, it shatters with a satisfying crunch.

Breaking down the flavor:

  • Initial: The milk chocolate melts in your mouth, releasing a rich, nutty flavor — noticeably deeper thanks to the higher cocoa content
  • Middle: The wafer crunches as it breaks apart, adding a toasted cereal note — a fantastic contrast with the chocolate
  • Finish: A balanced aftertaste of mild sweetness and gentle bitterness — never cloying

The Australian version's strength is balance. It's not too sweet, not too bitter. The ratio of milk to cocoa is just right, making it easy to eat several in one sitting without getting tired of it.

Japanese Matcha Kit Kat

The Japanese Kit Kat I tried is a matcha (green tea) white chocolate version. At first you taste the slight bitterness of matcha, then the sweetness of white chocolate follows. It's a Japan-exclusive limited edition, and once you start eating it, it's hard to stop. The matcha powder uses real green tea, so the flavor tastes natural, not artificial.

Japan's Kit Kat lineup is famous for variety. More than 300 different flavors have been released there — wasabi, soy sauce, sweet potato, sake, even corn soup. The name "Kit Kat" also sounds like "kitto katsu" (meaning "surely win" in Japanese), making it a popular lucky charm for students before exams.

The appeal of matcha Kit Kat is the coexistence of bitterness and sweetness. The sweetness of white chocolate wraps around the astringency of the matcha, while the finish is clean and refreshing, with green tea aroma lingering on your palate. The chocolate coating is thinner and creamier than the Australian original.

Country-by-Country Comparison

Kit Kat's taste and texture vary dramatically by country since different manufacturers produce it under license.

South Korea (Lotte): Lotte's Kit Kat is the sweetest of the bunch. The wafer is thinner and the chocolate coating is lighter, giving it an airy texture. It's cheap and widely available at convenience stores.

Australia (Nestlé): Higher cocoa content gives it a deeper chocolate flavor. The wafer is noticeably crunchier and firmer. Available in 4-finger, 2-finger, and Chunky formats.

Japan (Nestlé): Endless limited-edition flavors are the biggest draw. The base flavor is similar to the Australian version, but seasonal and regional exclusives come out constantly. The packaging design is beautiful, making them popular as gifts.

United Kingdom (Nestlé): The closest to the original recipe. Similar to the Australian version but with a slightly softer texture. A 4-finger bar is about 233 kcal, on par with other countries' versions.

United States (Hershey): Hershey produces Kit Kat under license in the US, and the recipe is noticeably different. The chocolate has a waxier texture and is much sweeter. A chunky version called "Big Kat" is also sold.

Overall Verdict

Kit Kat is more than just a chocolate bar — it's a global snack with a different personality in every country, which makes it genuinely fascinating. The Australian original delivers a clean, classic flavor profile that's hard to beat, while the Japanese matcha version impresses with its innovative and harmonious taste.

Personally, I find myself reaching for the Australian original more often. It's perfect with coffee or milk at home. But the matcha version is a special treat for special occasions.

Texture-wise, the Australian original is crunchier, while the Japanese matcha version has a smoother, creamier chocolate coating. Which one you prefer really comes down to personal taste.

Would I buy it again? I'll pick up matcha Kit Kat every time I visit Japan. The Australian original I'll regularly order online.
