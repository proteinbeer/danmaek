Kit Kat Review: Japanese vs Australian Comparison

Kit Kat was born in Britain, but today it's sold in dozens of different flavors and forms around the world. Japan especially has turned it into a massive cultural phenomenon, releasing over 300 limited-edition flavors over the years. This time, I'm comparing a rich Nestlé Australian original with the iconic Japanese matcha Kit Kat.

The story goes back to 1935. A worker at the Rowntree's factory in York, UK, suggested a "chocolate bar you could pack in a lunchbox," and that simple idea became the blueprint for Kit Kat. It initially launched as "Rowntree's Chocolate Crisp" before being renamed in 1937. Nestlé then acquired Rowntree's in 1988, turning Kit Kat into the global powerhouse it is today.

In South Korea, you can easily find Kit Kats at any convenience store or supermarket. Unlike the US where it's licensed out, the Korean market receives direct imports from Nestlé's large-scale regional hubs (predominantly Nestlé Malaysia), offering a smooth, classic taste profile that many locals have grown up with.

First Impression

The first thing you notice when you open the wrapper is the gentle, comforting cocoa aroma mixed with sweet milk chocolate. The Australian version comes in the traditional red package, while the Japanese matcha one sports a vibrant green — sitting side by side, the color contrast is striking. The Australian Kit Kat is crafted by Nestlé Australia, while the Japanese branch has a unique heritage, starting as a 1970s joint venture between Rowntree and Fujiya before fully integrating under the Nestlé Japan umbrella.

Kit Kat's defining feature is its flawless wafer structure. Most chocolate bars are just solid blocks, but Kit Kat layers three light wafers coated in chocolate, giving it that signature crisp, shattery crunch. That delicate sound and texture when you bite into it is seriously addictive.

Australian Original Kit Kat

The Australian Kit Kat is a stellar representation of the classic Nestlé recipe. Compared to the standard imports found in Southeast Asia or Korea, the Australian version features a remarkably robust, high-quality milk chocolate coating. It feels slightly richer and carries a deep cocoa intensity that balances out the sugar. The wafer layers inside feel firm and crisp, creating a satisfyingly loud snap when broken apart.

Breaking down the flavor:

  • Initial: The milk chocolate melts smoothly, releasing a rich, slightly nutty cocoa flavor with an elevated profile
  • Middle: The crisp wafer shatters, adding a clean, toasted cereal note that beautifully cuts the sweetness
  • Finish: A well-balanced aftertaste of mild creaminess and a gentle cocoa depth — never cloying or overly waxy

The ultimate strength of the Australian version is its structural integrity and balance. The chocolate coating is thick and generous, ensuring that you get a solid hit of premium milk chocolate alongside the airy wafer.

Japanese Matcha Kit Kat

The Japanese variant I tested is the world-famous Matcha (Green Tea) version. Upon the first bite, you are greeted by the distinct, authentic earthiness of real green tea leaves, followed immediately by the smooth sweetness of a premium white chocolate base. It's a flavor profile that has captivated tourists worldwide, turning a humble chocolate bar into a must-buy souvenir.

Japan's Kit Kat lineup is legendary for its experimental nature. Thanks to regional marketing, they have launched everything from wasabi and sake to sweet potato and Tokyo-exclusive variants. The name "Kit Kat" also happens to sound incredibly similar to the Japanese phrase "Kitto Katsu" (きっと勝つ), which translates to "surely win." This clever linguistic coincidence has turned the candy into a massive good-luck charm given to students right before exam season.

The true appeal of the Matcha Kit Kat lies in the brilliant juxtaposition of bitterness and sweetness. The creamy white chocolate base rounds out the natural astringency of the green tea, leaving a incredibly clean, aromatic finish on the palate. The chocolate layer here feels slightly thinner and creamier, designed to melt faster than the western counterparts.

Country-by-Country Comparison

Because Nestlé adapts its ingredients and licensing agreements to suit regional dairy profiles and climates, the global Kit Kat landscape is surprisingly varied:

South Korea (Imported Hubs): Typically sourced from regional Nestlé hubs like Malaysia. It leans into a sweeter, highly accessible milk chocolate profile with an incredibly light, airy wafer texture.

Australia (Nestlé AU): Features a thicker, premium chocolate coating with a higher cocoa depth. The biscuit wafer feels notably crunchier and firmer, holding up perfectly in hot weather.

Japan (Nestlé Japan): Centered entirely around culinary innovation and beautiful regional packaging. The chocolate textures lean creamier, serving as a canvas for hundreds of seasonal flavors.

United Kingdom (Nestlé UK): The spiritual home of the brand, utilizing a traditional recipe that delivers a very smooth, classic British milk chocolate finish with a slightly softer snap.

United States (Hershey's License): Manufactured under a long-standing license by The Hershey Company. The recipe uses a distinct American milk chocolate formulation, resulting in a noticeably sweeter taste and a slightly different mouthfeel.

Overall Verdict

Kit Kat is far more than a simple chocolate bar; it is a fascinating case study in global snack customization. The Australian original delivers a flawless, structurally superior classic flavor profile, while the Japanese Matcha version showcases how an international brand can masterfully absorb local culinary traditions.

For daily snacking alongside a hot black coffee, the hearty crunch of the Australian original remains incredibly hard to beat. However, if you are looking for a unique, aromatic, and deeply satisfying flavor journey, the Japanese Matcha version is an absolute masterpiece.

Would I buy it again? Absolutely. I will continue to order the Australian imports for my pantry, and the Japanese variants will always remain a mandatory purchase whenever I travel.

Where to buy: Hypermarkets, Asian specialty grocers, international convenience stores, and online via Coupang Global or Amazon.
