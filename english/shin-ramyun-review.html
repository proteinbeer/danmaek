Nongshim Shin Ramyun Review: The King of Korean Instant Noodles

You can't talk about Korean food without mentioning Shin Ramyun. First released in 1986 by Nongshim, this spicy beef-flavored instant noodle has become a global phenomenon. It's exported to over 100 countries and is a staple in every Korean household. I grew up on this stuff, so let me give you the honest scoop.

Shin Ramyun wasn't the first instant noodle in Korea (that was Samyang in 1963), but it quickly became the dominant brand. The name "Shin" (?) means "spicy" in Korean, and it lives up to that name. Over the past 35+ years, it has spawned multiple variants � Shin Black (premium), Shin Light (less spicy), and Shin Fried Noodles (a drier version).

First Impressions

The packet is iconic � the bold red packaging with the fierce "Shin" branding. Inside you get the noodle block, a powder soup base, and a separate vegetable/flake packet with dried shiitake mushrooms, green onions, and chili flakes. The noodle block is noticeably thicker than your average Maruchan or Top Ramen � Nongshim uses potato starch in the dough for that signature chewy texture.

The powder soup base is where the magic happens. It's made from beef bone extract, soy sauce, garlic, onion, and a proprietary blend of spices. When you add it to boiling water, the aroma fills the entire kitchen � savory, beefy, with a hint of heat that tells you this isn't your average noodle cup.

The vegetable flake packet includes dried shiitake mushrooms (which rehydrate beautifully), green onion flakes, carrot bits, and generous red chili flakes. I always add a bit extra at the end for color and crunch.

Taste and Heat Level

The broth is where Shin Ramyun shines. It's deeply savory with a serious beef umami kick, balanced by a slow-building heat that keeps you coming back for more. The spice level is medium by Korean standards � think jalape�o level, not ghost pepper. But if you're not used to spicy food, it'll definitely make your nose run.

Let me break down the flavor profile:

  • Base: Rich beef bone broth with garlic and onion � savory and deeply satisfying
  • Heat: Builds gradually from the gochugaru (Korean red pepper flakes) � not overwhelming, but persistent
  • Finish: A lingering warmth in the throat, with a touch of mushroom earthiness

I like to crack an egg into the boiling broth, add some sliced green onions, and throw in a slice of American cheese. The cheese melts into the broth and cuts the spice beautifully � a classic Korean move. For the full experience, add some dumplings (mandu) on the side.

Noodles: The Unsung Hero

The noodles deserve their own section. Unlike the thin, wavy noodles in many instant ramen brands, Shin Ramyun's noodles are thick, round, and springy. The potato starch in the dough gives them a bouncy, chewy texture that holds up even after several minutes in the boiling broth. They don't get mushy or soggy � even if you forget about them for an extra minute.

The recommended cooking time is 4-5 minutes, but I find 4 minutes gives the perfect al dente texture. The noodles should still have a slight resistance when you bite into them. If you're adding an egg, drop it in at the 3-minute mark and let it poach gently.

Shin Ramyun vs Buldak

Compared to Samyang Buldak, Shin Ramyun is less aggressive. Buldak is a spice challenge; Shin Ramyun is a comforting daily meal. Buldak's heat hits hard and fast, while Shin's builds gradually and feels more integrated with the broth. If Buldak is a firework, Shin Ramyun is a campfire � warm, steady, and familiar.

Shin Ramyun also has a more complex broth. Buldak is primarily about the spicy sauce, while Shin Ramyun offers layers of beef, garlic, and mushroom flavors. They're both excellent, but I reach for Shin when I want comfort, and Buldak when I want a thrill.

Variants Worth Trying

Shin Black: The premium version. It uses a richer beef bone broth base, adds garlic oil, and includes a packet of real vegetable chunks. Less spicy, more complex. Often considered the best instant noodle in the world.

Shin Light: Lower calorie version with less sodium and a milder spice level. Good if you want the Shin flavor without the heavy sodium load.

Shin Fried Noodles: A dry version where the noodles are boiled, then stir-fried with a spicy sauce. No broth � just chewy noodles coated in a concentrated Shin-flavored sauce. Great for a change of pace.

Nutrition

One packet (120g) contains about 500 calories, 16g of fat (8g saturated), 80g of carbs, and a hefty 1,690mg of sodium. It's not health food � it's comfort food. The sodium is the biggest concern, so I often use only 3/4 of the soup powder and add more vegetables to bulk it up.

Compared to other instant noodles, Shin is average in calories but higher in sodium. The protein content (10g per packet) is decent because of the wheat and potato starch blend.

Verdict

Shin Ramyun is a classic for a reason. It's not the trendiest or the spiciest, but it's reliable, satisfying, and consistently good. Whether you're a broke college student or a Korean food enthusiast, this belongs in your pantry.

What makes it special is the balance � the heat is present but not punishing, the broth is savory without being heavy, and the noodles are chewy without being tough. It's a product that has been refined over 35 years, and it shows in every bite.

Would I buy again? Always. It's my go-to comfort food. I buy the 20-pack from H Mart and always have a stash.

Where to buy: Every Korean grocery store, Amazon, Walmart (international aisle), H Mart, Weee!
